GAME ASSETS
  • I’m very proud of the work I’ve put into Titan so far. I definitely bit off more than I could chew all at once with this project, but it has been a huge opportunity to identify where I went wrong and to take away something useful from those mistakes.

    I now have practice determining a reasonable scope for a project, setting up goals for the completion of manageable segments, improved time management, and many other strategies for managing a larger project. Most importantly though I’ve learned about realistically identifying a minimum viable product. How to look for where the cuts can be made, and how to make sure those cuts can be made while leaving the underlying product intact.
  • Facade was shipped in CS:GO Operation Vanguard at the end of 2014 to about 6.5 million monthly unique players. Aside from being an honor to have my work included in a popular AAA title, it was an opportunity to learn about shipping a product to a larger audience.

    Before Valve launched their map pack facade had a respectable 34 thousand unique subscribers. The type of attention I got at those numbers resulted in a great feedback cycle. However, being on the radar of 6.5 million users was a different experience all together. Within minutes of the operation launching there were youtube videos and forum posts of people finding bugs and small exploits. Stuff that we missed after hundreds of hours of testing by us and by Valve. It made me aware of the implications of millions of eyes on one product. There is simply a different standard at that level, one which requires painstaking attention to detail in order to be achieved.
  • The WibiData project was commissioned as a hiring tool to be used by WibiData (a data analytics company) as a job application for new hires. The idea was to build a small campaign of puzzle levels based in a rebranded version of the popular puzzle game "Portal 2". My responsibility for the project was to create a single coop multiplayer level that would be satisfying to players looking for multiplayer experiences without expending too many resources on several multiplayer levels.

    I decided to accomplish this by creating a large multi-part puzzle and adding a rug pull where players thought the level might end. This turned the supposed endpoint into a checkpoint before an unexpected final large puzzle. The result of the large puzzle opening up to become even larger was a sense of scale which matched that of several levels while only requiring the creation of one large one.
  • Labrat is an unfinished project that's always in the back of my head. It was initially meant to be a mod for Portal 2 where you play as a scientist character from the lore of the games. A lot of work was put into the project at this stage, but as a more amateur project a lack of direction ultimately caused it to fizzle out.

    Even though the project was dead in the water I was enamored by the idea of the protagonist. This character was schizophrenic, he was trapped without the medicine that helped him cope with his illness, and the place he was trapped was in itself otherworldly. The line between reality and what was in his mind was a complete blur. I love the dark possibilities of that core concept and I'm slowly designing a game that uses it as its core gameplay mechanic.

Max Giddens | Level Designer
Creating worlds for people to experience and explore has been my passion for the past decade. I’ve played video games for as long as I can remember, but starting with Halo 3’s “forge mode” I became increasingly interested in how they are made. I eagerly toiled away in the clunky editor to create intersting mini games and competitive multiplayer maps inamered with the experience of building something that people would interact with. I knew then that I wanted to dive deeper into this world.

Since then I have picked up whatever new skill would help me build bigger and better projects. Modeling, texturing, color and lighting psychology. Whatever I can add to my tool belt to keep growing as a designer.

My drive has led me to the point that my work has been recognized by sites like PC Gamer and included in CS:GO. I love what I do and I’m excited to continue expanding my skills and creating more complex and engaging projects.
